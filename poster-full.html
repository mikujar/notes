Problem: Notes Scatter, Stories Get Lost
Note-taking in daily life starts with fragmented thoughts, reminders, and temporary ideas. While existing tools are effective for structured information, they are less suited to capturing and revisiting scattered note fragments.
Fragmented capture No topic continuity Lost context over time
As notes accumulate, entries related to the same topic — a person, an event, a project — become separated across time, making it hard to see the bigger picture.
Inspired by the DIKWI Model
The DIKWI model shows how scattered pieces can be gradually transformed into connected understanding.

Based on this idea, Mikujar is designed to support the transition from isolated fragments to connected understanding over time.
"Life is not a line — but your notes can reveal one."

Chronicle Tracking — Design & Insight
How the system turns a pile of time-stamped notes into a living record of people, events, and ideas.
🔩 How It Works
1
Subject Extraction — The system identifies named subjects (people, places, projects) from note content using keyword matching and user-defined labels.
2
Dual-Axis Indexing — Each note is indexed along two axes: when it was written and what subject it belongs to, forming a sparse two-dimensional map.
3
Thread Assembly — Notes sharing the same subject are pulled into a chronological thread — a "Chronicle" — regardless of which collection they live in.
4
Change Detection — The system compares sequential entries in a thread to surface shifts in tone, status, or frequency, flagging significant changes for the user to review.
💡 Insight Examples
Relationship drift You wrote about Alice frequently in January, then stopped. Mikujar surfaces this silence as a notable gap in the thread.
Project momentum Notes on "Project X" cluster around deadlines. The system highlights bursts of activity and extended quiet periods.
Recurring pattern The same concern appears in notes three months apart. Mikujar links the dots and asks: is this resolved, or still open?
Convergence Two separate subjects — a person and a project — begin appearing together in notes. The system suggests a shared Chronicle.
